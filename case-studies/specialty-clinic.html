The Client

The Snapshot

A specialty podiatry and medical foot care clinic in Central Florida with three clinic locations across the region. Around 50 employees — physicians, podology technicians, call center staff, clinic coordinators, admin, and support.

The clinic operated 12 treatment rooms, with 6–7 active on a typical day, generating 35–50 appointments daily. Marketing campaigns brought in 50–100 new patient consultations monthly.

Paul built the clinic into a respected medical practice over the years. The clinical work was excellent — diabetic ulcers, fungal infections, biomechanical issues, custom orthotics. Real medical podology, not cosmetic foot care.

Software: a CRM for lead management, scheduling software, and spreadsheets for everything that fell between. The tools existed. The system didn't.

The Situation

What Was Actually Happening

The Patient Drop-Off Problem

"We'd see a patient three or four times. The condition would start improving. Then they'd disappear. No cancellation, no call — just gone. Three months later, same patient, same problem, worse than before. We were treating the same conditions twice because patients didn't finish the first time."

The clinic estimated that only 55% of patients completed their full treatment protocol. Nearly half stopped prematurely — not because the treatment wasn't working, but because it was. Once the pain decreased, so did the urgency.

The Lead Response Decay

"Leads came in from ads, from the website, from referrals. They went into the CRM. But if the call team was busy, those leads might sit for a day or two. By then, the patient had either booked somewhere else or decided they could live with the problem a little longer."

In specialty care, patient urgency is perishable. A patient inquiring while in pain is not the same person 48 hours later. Speed wasn't a convenience metric. It was a conversion multiplier. The clinic was responding to cooled-down leads and wondering why conversion rates were inconsistent.

The Education Gap

"Patients would ask 'how much does it cost?' before they understood what they actually needed. They thought this was like a pedicure — one visit, problem solved. When we explained it might take four to six sessions, some were surprised. Others were skeptical. A few walked out."

Many patients didn't understand the difference between medical podology and cosmetic foot care. They expected instant results. When the clinic explained that real healing requires time and compliance, some patients questioned whether multiple sessions were really necessary — even though the protocol was medically necessary.

The Visibility Lag

"We reviewed numbers monthly, sometimes quarterly. By the time we noticed a problem — a drop in retention, a bad campaign — it had already cost us weeks of patients. We were always reacting, never anticipating."

The clinic had data, but it arrived too late to act on. Problems were discovered in retrospective reports, not in real-time dashboards. By the time leadership saw the pattern, the window to intervene had closed.

The Owner Trap

"I built this clinic. I know every corner of it. But somewhere along the way, I became the person who has to touch every decision. If I'm not there, things slow down. If I am there, I'm overwhelmed. I don't run the business anymore — I just hold it together."

Paul had become the integrator of everything — operations, marketing, staffing, patient issues, vendor decisions. Not because his team was incapable, but because the systems didn't exist to let them operate independently. Trust was low because visibility was low. When the system can't show what's happening, the owner becomes the system.

“

We used to lose patients after the third session. They'd feel better and disappear. Now the system flags it automatically the moment they miss. My team makes one call, the patient comes back, they actually finish treatment. Better outcomes for them, more revenue for us. Everybody wins.

— Paul, Owner, Specialty Clinic
What We Installed

The Systems

Instead of replacing the clinic's existing tools, we built an operational layer that connected lead intake, patient education, treatment adherence, and executive visibility into a single system.

01

Lead Response Automation

Every inquiry — web form, ad response, referral — triggers immediate follow-up. No lead sits waiting for a free call agent. Response time dropped from 24–72 hours to under 2 hours.

02

Patient Education Sequence

Before the first consultation, patients receive clear information about what medical podology actually is, why treatment takes multiple sessions, and what happens if they stop early. Expectations are set before they walk in the door.

03

Treatment Plan Commitment Flow

At the initial consultation, patients are walked through their specific protocol — number of sessions, timeline, what improvement looks like vs. what completion looks like. They leave with a clear treatment plan, not just a next appointment.

04

Treatment Adherence Tracker

Every patient's protocol status is visible. If someone misses a session or is overdue, the system flags it immediately. Staff can intervene in days, not months.

05

Reactivation System

Patients who dropped off 60+ days ago are identified and contacted. Not a generic reminder — a personalized re-engagement acknowledging where they left off and what's recommended next.

06

Live Ops Dashboard

Real-time visibility into daily operations: leads in queue, consultations booked, appointments completed, patients at risk of dropping. No more waiting for monthly reports to see what's happening.

07

Marketing & Revenue Attribution

Every booked patient tied back to their source. For the first time, the clinic could see which campaigns produced patients who actually completed treatment — not just leads, but real revenue.

08 Core System

Executive Daily Brief

Paul's morning summary: what needs attention, what's overdue, what's coming this week. A decision queue, not an information flood. Thirty minutes of structured review replaced hours of reactive firefighting.

The Stories Behind the Numbers

The Protocol That Finally Got Finished

A diabetic patient with recurring ulcers had visited the clinic three separate times over two years — each time starting treatment, improving, then disappearing. With the adherence tracker, staff noticed when he missed his third session. A follow-up call brought him back. He completed the protocol for the first time. No recurrence since.

The Lead That Didn't Go Cold

A patient submitted an inquiry on a Saturday afternoon. Old system: it would sit until Monday, maybe Tuesday. New system: automated response within minutes, call scheduled for Monday morning. She booked the consultation. Later said she'd almost gone to a competitor over the weekend.

The Education That Prevented the Walkout

A new patient came in expecting a one-time treatment. When the doctor explained a four-session protocol, she hesitated — "That sounds like a lot." But she'd already received the education sequence. She understood why. She completed all four sessions and left a five-star review mentioning "they explained everything upfront."

The Campaign That Wasn't Working

Attribution tracking revealed that one ad campaign was generating leads but almost no completed treatments. The patients it attracted had wrong expectations. The clinic paused that campaign and reallocated budget. Cost per completed patient dropped significantly once the clinic stopped paying for the wrong expectations.

The Sunday Morning Test

Paul tracked his time for a month. Before: checking messages constantly, piecing together updates from three different sources, answering questions that shouldn't need him. After: one structured morning briefing. Sundays are quiet. Emergencies still come through. Everything else has an owner.

“

My favorite part? I stopped being the person who holds everything in his head. The systems hold it now. I just make decisions.

— Paul, Owner, Specialty Clinic
