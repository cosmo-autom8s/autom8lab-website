The Client

The Snapshot

A general contractor in Phoenix, Arizona, running commercial tenant improvements and light industrial projects. $8M annual revenue, 23 employees, a mix of project managers, superintendents, field crews, and one office admin who handles "everything else."

The owner, Marcus, started as a carpenter 19 years ago. Built his reputation on quality work and showing up when he said he would. Now he runs a company where he's the bottleneck for every decision, and he hasn't touched a tool in 3 years.

Software: Procore for project management (half-adopted), QuickBooks for accounting, Excel for everything that falls between, and a group text thread per project that never stops buzzing.

The Situation

What Was Actually Happening

The Change Order Black Hole

"We had a $2.1M tenant improvement job last year. Finished it, felt good. Then I sat down with my accountant and realized we'd eaten $180K in scope changes we never billed for. Not because the client refused. Because we never sent the change orders. They were sitting in email threads and text messages. Nobody tracked them."

Marcus wasn't losing money on bad work. He was losing it on good work that never got invoiced.

The RFI Bottleneck

"An RFI comes in from the field. It goes to the PM. The PM emails the architect. The architect takes 5 days. Meanwhile, the crew is either waiting or guessing. Last month we had a framing crew standing around for two days because nobody knew which spec was current. That's $6,400 in labor we'll never get back."

It's not just proposals that kill schedules. It's the daily back-and-forth. RFIs without owners, without deadlines, without tracking.

The Payment Float Trap

"I've got $340K out right now in invoices over 30 days. One GC I sub for takes 67 days average to pay. So I'm floating payroll, floating materials, floating everything. Last month I pulled from my personal line of credit to make Friday's checks. That's not a business. That's a stress machine."

The average contractor is financing their client's project out of their own pocket, and the margins are already razor-thin at 2–6%.

The Owner Trap

"I started this company because I'm good at building. Somewhere along the way I became the guy who approves every PO, answers every question, and puts out every fire. I've got two PMs but they still call me for everything. I don't run the business anymore. It runs me."

Marcus was working 60+ hours a week, and half of it was admin he shouldn't be touching.

“

Every scope change used to disappear into a text thread. Now it's tracked from the moment someone mentions it in the field. That's not a process improvement. That's money we were already owed finally making it to the invoice.

— Marcus, Owner, GC Firm
What We Installed

The Systems

01

Change Order Capture System

Every scope change, whether from the field, client, or architect, enters one queue. Auto-generates CO draft with cost estimate. Nothing gets built until it's approved and tracked.

02

RFI Tracker with SLA

Every RFI has an owner, a deadline, and a status. Architects get automatic reminders. PMs see what's pending without digging through email. Escalates to Marcus only when something's overdue.

03

Document Version Control

One source of truth for specs, drawings, and submittals. Field crews see current version on their phone. No more "I was working off the old drawing."

04

Payment Tracking Dashboard

Every invoice tracked from sent to paid. Aging alerts at 30/60/90 days. Marcus sees cash position weekly without asking the accountant. Flags clients who consistently pay slow.

05

Daily Field Log Automation

Supers submit daily logs from their phone. Auto-tagged to project, auto-filed, searchable. Documentation that used to take 45 minutes takes 10.

06

Subcontractor Coordination Queue

All sub-related tasks in one place. Who's scheduled, who confirmed, who didn't show. No more chasing subs via text at 6am.

07

Weekly Project Status Report

Auto-generated from project data. Sent every Monday to stakeholders. Clients see progress without calling Marcus. He stopped being the status update hotline.

08 Core System

Owner's Daily Brief

Morning summary: what needs approval, what's overdue, what's coming this week. Decision queue, not information flood. Marcus makes decisions in 30 minutes instead of discovering them throughout the day.

The Stories Behind the Numbers

The $47K Recovery

First 60 days, the change order system captured 11 scope changes that would have gone unbilled under the old process. Total value: $47,200. That's not new revenue. It's revenue they were already earning but not collecting.

The RFI That Didn't Kill the Schedule

Week 3, an electrical spec conflict came in from the field. Old system: PM emails architect, waits, follows up, waits more, crew guesses. New system: RFI logged, architect auto-notified with 48-hour SLA, response came in 31 hours, crew never stopped. Estimated savings: $4,800 in avoided downtime.

The Sub Who Finally Showed Up

Marcus had a drywall sub who no-showed twice in one month. Nobody tracked it until the super complained. With the coordination queue, the pattern was visible in week 1. They had a conversation, set expectations, sub's been reliable since. Before: problem discovered after damage. After: problem visible before it compounds.

The Payment That Actually Came

One client was averaging 67 days to pay. Payment dashboard flagged them at day 35 with automatic follow-up. Accounting sent a reminder. Payment came day 41. Over 3 invoices, that's roughly $23K that came 3–4 weeks faster than it would have.

The Saturday Morning Test

Marcus tracked his time for a month. Before: checking texts by 6am, including weekends. Approving POs on Saturday, answering "quick questions" on Sunday. After: Saturday check-in is 15 minutes. Real emergencies still come through. Everything else has an owner and a system.

“

I started this company because I know how to build. For the last five years, all I've done is answer emails and approve POs. Now the system handles the noise and I'm back to doing what actually grows the business.

— Marcus, Owner, GC Firm
